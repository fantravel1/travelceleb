Why Creators Love Peru

The land of ancient wonders that 800+ travel creators cannot stop filming

Peru is a country that defies singular definition. It is Machu Picchu at dawn, clouds parting to reveal the most recognizable archaeological site on Earth — a lost Inca citadel clinging to a ridge above the Urubamba River, its terraces and stone temples emerging from the mist like a vision from another age. That single image has driven millions of views and inspired hundreds of creators to make the pilgrimage, but Machu Picchu is only the beginning of what makes Peru one of the most compelling destinations on the planet.

Lima has quietly become the culinary capital of the world. With three restaurants in the World's 50 Best — Central, Maido, and Kjolle — and a street food scene that rivals anywhere in Asia, the city is a pilgrimage for food creators. Ceviche, lomo saltado, anticuchos, and causa are just the opening course. The fusion of indigenous, Spanish, African, Chinese, and Japanese culinary traditions has produced a cuisine of extraordinary depth and originality that creators like Mark Wiens have dedicated entire series to exploring.

Beyond the citadels and kitchens, Peru contains staggering geographic diversity. The Amazon rainforest covers over sixty percent of the country, offering jungle lodges, wildlife encounters, and indigenous communities that few other destinations can match. The Andes form the spine of the nation, with snow-capped peaks above 6,000 meters, the surreal Rainbow Mountain of Vinicunca painted in bands of mineral color, and the Colca Canyon — twice as deep as the Grand Canyon — where Andean condors ride thermal updrafts at eye level. Lake Titicaca, the highest navigable lake in the world, hosts floating islands built entirely from totora reeds by the Uros people, a living tradition that has persisted for centuries.

For creators, Peru offers something rare: the ability to film genuinely once-in-a-lifetime moments across radically different landscapes within a single trip. The Sacred Valley between Cusco and Machu Picchu is a corridor of Inca ruins, traditional markets, and terraced mountainsides that photographs like nowhere else. The Nazca Lines remain one of the great mysteries of the ancient world. And the warmth of Peruvian people — their pride in their heritage, their generosity to visitors — gives every video an authenticity that resonates deeply with audiences worldwide.

Must-Visit Locations

Peru spans coast, mountains, and jungle — each region a different world

Cusco

The former capital of the Inca Empire, sitting at 3,400 meters in the Andes. Cobblestone streets lined with Inca stone walls, colonial churches built atop temples, vibrant San Pedro Market, and the gateway to the Sacred Valley and Machu Picchu.

Inca Capital

Machu Picchu

The iconic 15th-century Inca citadel perched at 2,430 meters above sea level. A UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World — the single most filmed location in all of South America.

World Wonder

Lima

Peru's coastal capital and the undisputed culinary capital of South America. World-class restaurants, the bohemian Barranco district, Miraflores clifftop parks, ancient Huaca pyramids, and a food scene that rivals any city on Earth.

Culinary Capital

Sacred Valley

The fertile valley between Cusco and Machu Picchu, dotted with Inca ruins at Ollantaytambo and Pisac, traditional weaving villages, the Moray circular terraces, and the striking Maras salt mines used since Inca times.

Heritage

Rainbow Mountain

Vinicunca, the Rainbow Mountain, rises to 5,200 meters with bands of red, yellow, green, and turquoise mineral deposits striping its slopes. A challenging high-altitude day hike from Cusco that has become one of Peru's most photographed sights.

Photography

Lake Titicaca

The highest navigable lake in the world at 3,812 meters, straddling the Peru-Bolivia border. Home to the Uros floating islands made of totora reeds, the island of Taquile with its textile traditions, and vast deep-blue waters ringed by snow-capped peaks.

Culture

Top Activities

The experiences that creators film most in Peru

Inca Trail Trek

The legendary 4-day, 43-kilometer trek through cloud forests and Inca ruins to the Sun Gate overlooking Machu Picchu. Limited to 500 people per day — permits sell out months in advance. The ultimate South American hiking experience.

Must-Do $500–1,500

Lima Food Tours

Explore the culinary capital of South America — from cevicherias and street-side anticuchos to fine-dining at Central (ranked #1 in the world). Taste lomo saltado, causa, and pisco sours in Miraflores and Barranco.

Food & Culture $50–150

Amazon Expeditions

Multi-day jungle expeditions from Iquitos or Puerto Maldonado. Spot jaguars, macaws, pink river dolphins, and caimans. Stay in eco-lodges deep in the rainforest and visit indigenous communities. Peru's Amazon is among the most biodiverse places on Earth.

Wildlife $200–600

Sandboarding Huacachina

A desert oasis surrounded by towering sand dunes in southern Peru. Ride dune buggies at breakneck speed, then sandboard down massive slopes at sunset. One of the most unexpected and thrilling experiences in South America.

Adventure $30–60

Colca Canyon

Twice as deep as the Grand Canyon, Colca Canyon is one of the deepest in the world. Hike to oasis villages at the canyon floor, soak in natural hot springs, and watch Andean condors with three-meter wingspans soar at eye level from Cruz del Condor viewpoint.

Nature $20–80

Best Time to Visit

Peru's diverse geography means timing depends on where you go

Dry Season (May–Oct)

The ideal time for the Andes, Machu Picchu, and the Inca Trail. Clear skies, cold nights, and sunny days in the highlands. June through August is peak season with the best visibility but also the largest crowds and highest prices. September-October offers similar weather with fewer tourists.

Wet Season (Nov–Apr)

Afternoon rains in the highlands, but mornings are often clear. Lush green landscapes and far fewer tourists. The Inca Trail closes in February for maintenance. Lima's coast is actually sunnier during these months. The Amazon is wet year-round but navigable rivers make boat travel easier December through May.

Creator Tip

Most creators recommend May or September-October as the sweet spot. You get dry-season conditions in the Andes with shoulder-season pricing, thinner crowds at Machu Picchu, and the best light for photography. Arrive in Cusco two days early to acclimatize before trekking — altitude sickness is real at 3,400 meters.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know before visiting Peru

How many days do you need for Peru?

A minimum of 10 to 14 days is recommended for Peru's highlights. Spend 2-3 days in Lima for the food scene, 2-3 days in Cusco for acclimatization and exploration, 1-2 days in the Sacred Valley, and 1-2 days at Machu Picchu. The Inca Trail adds 4 days, and an Amazon excursion requires 3-4 more. For Rainbow Mountain, Lake Titicaca, or Huacachina, plan 16-21 days total. Do not rush from sea level to Cusco — altitude acclimatization is essential at 3,400 meters.

Is Peru safe for tourists?

Peru is generally safe for tourists, especially along the main travel corridors of Lima (Miraflores and Barranco), Cusco, the Sacred Valley, and Machu Picchu. Use common-sense precautions: avoid displaying expensive items, use registered taxis or ride-hailing apps, and stay alert in crowded markets and bus stations. Altitude sickness is a bigger concern than crime for most travelers — Cusco sits at 3,400 meters. Stay hydrated, take it slow your first day, and try coca tea. Travel insurance is highly recommended.

What is the best time to visit Machu Picchu?

The best time to visit Machu Picchu is during the dry season from May to October. June through August offers the clearest skies and best visibility, though these are the busiest months. September and October provide excellent weather with noticeably fewer crowds. The wet season (November to March) brings daily rain and fog that can obscure views, but also far fewer tourists. February is the wettest month and the Inca Trail is closed for maintenance. Book entry tickets and Inca Trail permits months in advance regardless of season.

How much does a trip to Peru cost?

Peru offers excellent value for travelers. Budget travelers can explore on $40-60 USD per day, staying in hostels, eating at local markets, and using public buses. Mid-range travelers spend $100-200 per day with comfortable hotels, guided tours, and restaurant dining. Luxury travelers enjoy premium lodges, private guides, and Lima's world-class restaurants for $300-800+ per day. The Inca Trail costs $500-1,500 depending on the operator. Machu Picchu entry is approximately $50 USD. Domestic flights between Lima and Cusco run $60-150.
