Why Creators Love Portugal

The country that 1,000+ travel creators cannot stop filming

Portugal has become the undisputed darling of the travel creator world in Europe, and the reasons run far deeper than its photogenic azulejo tiles. At the intersection of old-world charm and modern creative energy, Portugal offers a quality of life that rivals any country on the continent at a fraction of the cost. Lisbon's nightlife pulses through cobblestone alleys where centuries-old fado bars sit alongside rooftop cocktail lounges overlooking the Tagus River. Porto's Ribeira waterfront, a UNESCO World Heritage site, provides a backdrop so cinematic that creators joke it was designed specifically for content. The Algarve's golden sea caves and dramatic limestone cliffs have become some of the most recognizable landscapes on social media.

But Portugal's appeal to creators goes beyond aesthetics. The country pioneered the Digital Nomad Visa in Europe, actively courting remote workers and content creators with streamlined residency processes and favorable tax regimes. Lisbon's co-working scene rivals Berlin and Bali, with spaces like Second Home and Outsite becoming creative hubs where travel YouTubers, photographers, and writers cross-pollinate ideas over excellent Portuguese coffee. The cost of living remains remarkably affordable for Western Europe — a leisurely dinner with wine in Lisbon costs what a single cocktail does in London or Paris.

Surfing is woven into Portugal's identity in a way no other European country can match. Nazare produces the biggest waves on the planet — towering walls of water that have drawn big-wave surfers and filmmakers from around the globe. Ericeira, Europe's only World Surfing Reserve, offers consistent breaks for every level. Peniche and the Algarve coast add still more variety. For creators, the combination of world-class surf footage and Mediterranean warmth is irresistible. And then there are the hidden treasures: the fairy-tale palaces of Sintra shrouded in mist, the volcanic landscapes of the Azores that feel like another planet, the subtropical gardens of Madeira floating in the Atlantic. Portugal packs an extraordinary diversity of experiences into a country smaller than the state of Indiana — and does it all with warmth, simplicity, and a pastel de nata in hand.

Must-Visit Locations

Each region of Portugal offers a completely different experience

Lisbon

Portugal's sun-drenched capital built on seven hills. Wander the labyrinthine alleys of Alfama, ride the iconic Tram 28, explore the vibrant Time Out Market, and watch the sunset from one of countless miradouros overlooking terracotta rooftops and the Tagus.

Capital City

Porto

The romantic northern city straddling the Douro River. Home to UNESCO-listed Ribeira, stunning azulejo-covered churches, the legendary Livraria Lello bookshop, and the port wine cellars of Vila Nova de Gaia. A food lover's paradise.

Wine Country

Algarve

Portugal's stunning southern coastline. Golden limestone cliffs, sea caves reached by kayak, hidden coves with turquoise water, and charming fishing villages. The Benagil Cave is one of the most photographed spots in all of Europe.

Beach Paradise

Sintra

A fairy-tale town just 30 minutes from Lisbon. The colourful Pena Palace perched on misty hilltops, the mysterious Quinta da Regaleira with its Initiation Well, lush forests, and Moorish castle ruins overlooking the Atlantic coast.

Fairy Tale

Azores

Nine volcanic islands in the mid-Atlantic offering otherworldly landscapes. Crater lakes in every shade of green and blue, natural hot springs, whale watching, and emerald pastures — often called the Hawaii of Europe.

Island Paradise

Madeira

The floating garden of the Atlantic. Dramatic mountain peaks, levada walking trails through laurel forests, year-round subtropical climate, and the legendary Belmond Reid's Palace. Madeira is Portugal's best-kept luxury secret.

Adventure

Where Creators Stay

The most-featured luxury hotels and resorts in Portugal

Belmond Reid's Palace, Madeira

Perched on a cliff above Funchal since 1891, this legendary palace hotel has hosted Winston Churchill, George Bernard Shaw, and generations of royalty. Subtropical gardens cascade toward the Atlantic, and afternoon tea on the terrace is a Portuguese institution.

From EUR 450/night ★ 9.6

Pestana Palace, Lisbon

A 19th-century palace turned five-star hotel in Lisbon's elegant Alcantara district. Lavish frescoed ceilings, manicured French gardens, an outdoor pool framed by palm trees, and one of the finest breakfast spreads in Europe. Pure Portuguese grandeur.

From EUR 320/night ★ 9.4

Six Senses Douro Valley

A beautifully restored 19th-century manor overlooking the terraced vineyards of the Douro Valley, a UNESCO World Heritage landscape. Wine tastings, spa treatments using local botanicals, and some of the most breathtaking river views in Europe.

From EUR 500/night ★ 9.7

Vila Vita Parc, Algarve

A sprawling clifftop resort on the Algarve coast featuring private beach access, Michelin-starred dining at Ocean Restaurant, lush tropical gardens, and a yacht available for coastal cruises. The definitive Algarve luxury experience.

From EUR 380/night ★ 9.5

Top Activities

The experiences that creators film most in Portugal

Surfing

From Nazare's record-breaking big waves to Ericeira's World Surfing Reserve and Peniche's consistent breaks. Portugal is Europe's undisputed surf capital with waves for every level and warm-water seasons from June to October.

Must-Do From EUR 25

Wine Tasting

Cruise the Douro Valley vineyards, sample port wine in Vila Nova de Gaia's historic cellars, or discover the crisp vinho verde of the Minho region. Portugal has 14 wine regions producing some of Europe's most underrated vintages.

Culture EUR 15–60

Tram 28 Ride

Lisbon's iconic yellow tram rattling through the narrow streets of Alfama, Graca, and Baixa. A living piece of history and one of the most filmed transport experiences in Europe. Board early morning to avoid the crowds.

Iconic EUR 3

Pasteis de Nata Tours

Hunt for the perfect custard tart across Lisbon — from the legendary Pasteis de Belem (using a secret recipe since 1837) to modern artisan bakeries reinventing the classic. Every creator has their favourite spot.

Food EUR 1–2

Fado Shows

Experience Portugal's soul music in the intimate fado houses of Lisbon's Alfama and Mouraria districts. UNESCO-listed Intangible Cultural Heritage, fado's haunting melodies express saudade — a longing that defines Portuguese culture.

Music EUR 20–50

Cliff Walks

Hike the Seven Hanging Valleys Trail along the Algarve's golden limestone cliffs, explore Madeira's levada trails through ancient laurel forests, or walk the dramatic coastline of Rota Vicentina on the wild Atlantic coast.

Adventure Free

Best Time to Visit

Portugal is a year-round destination, but timing enhances the experience

Summer (Jun–Sep)

Peak season with the warmest weather — 25-35 degrees Celsius in the south, ideal for beaches and the Algarve. July and August are busiest with highest prices. Lisbon and Porto are hot but vibrant with outdoor festivals, rooftop bars, and late sunsets past 9 PM.

Shoulder (Apr–May, Oct)

The sweet spot for most creators. Warm and sunny days (20-26 degrees Celsius), thinner crowds, lower hotel prices, and the best light for photography. Ideal for Sintra, the Douro Valley, and city exploration without the summer heat.

Winter (Nov–Mar)

Mild in Lisbon and the Algarve (12-16 degrees Celsius), with occasional rain. The best big-wave surfing season at Nazare. Hotel prices drop significantly, tourist sites are empty, and the Azores and Madeira remain pleasantly subtropical year-round.

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know before visiting Portugal

What is the best time to visit Portugal?

The best time to visit Portugal is from late spring to early autumn — May through October. June to September offers the warmest weather with temperatures from 25 to 35 degrees Celsius in the south. July and August are peak tourist season with the highest prices. The shoulder months of May-June and September-October provide warm weather with fewer crowds and lower hotel rates. Winter (November-March) is mild in Lisbon and the Algarve and is ideal for surfing, cultural exploration, and budget travel.

Is Portugal good for digital nomads?

Portugal is widely considered the best digital nomad destination in Europe. Lisbon and Porto offer world-class co-working spaces, excellent high-speed internet, a thriving international community, and an affordable cost of living compared to other Western European capitals. Portugal's dedicated Digital Nomad Visa (D8) allows remote workers to live and work legally. The GMT/GMT+1 time zone is convenient for both American and European clients, and English is widely spoken throughout the country.

How much does a trip to Portugal cost?

Portugal is one of the most affordable countries in Western Europe. Budget travelers can enjoy Portugal on 50-70 EUR per day including hostels, local meals, and public transport. Mid-range travelers spend 100-180 EUR per day with boutique hotels, restaurant dining, and activities. Luxury travelers can enjoy palace hotels, Michelin-starred restaurants, and premium wine experiences for 300-600 EUR per day. A quality restaurant meal in Lisbon costs 10-15 EUR, excellent Portuguese wine is 3-5 EUR per glass, and public transport is very affordable.

Do I need a visa to visit Portugal?

Portugal is part of the Schengen Area. EU, EEA, and Swiss citizens can enter freely. Citizens of the US, Canada, UK, Australia, Japan, and many other countries can visit visa-free for up to 90 days within any 180-day period. Non-EU visitors from visa-exempt countries will need to register with ETIAS before traveling. For longer stays, Portugal offers a Digital Nomad Visa (D8), student visas, and the popular D7 passive income visa. Always check the latest requirements with the Portuguese embassy before traveling.
