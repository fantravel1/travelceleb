Why Bali Is #1 for Creators

The island that launched a thousand YouTube channels and redefined what it means to be a travel influencer.

There is no destination on Earth that has shaped the modern travel influencer movement quite like Bali. This Indonesian island, often called the "Island of the Gods," has become the undisputed capital of creator culture, a place where rice terraces cascade down volcanic hillsides, ancient Hindu temples perch on ocean cliffs, and world-class surf breaks roll in alongside some of the most photogenic sunsets you will ever witness. Over 2,400 travel creators have featured Bali in their content, generating more than 850 dedicated videos and half a billion combined views. Those numbers are not a coincidence. They reflect a destination that offers everything a content creator needs: jaw-dropping visuals, diverse experiences, affordable luxury, and a community of like-minded nomads from every corner of the globe.

The magic of Bali lies in its extraordinary range. Within a single island roughly the size of Delaware, you can wake up in a luxury jungle villa in Ubud, practice sunrise yoga overlooking the Tegallalang rice terraces, ride a scooter past centuries-old Hindu temples, grab a flat white at a Canggu cafe filled with remote workers, surf your first wave at Batu Bolong beach, and end the day watching the sun set behind the silhouette of Tanah Lot temple while sipping a cocktail at a cliffside bar. No other destination in the world packs this much diversity into such a compact space. The combination of Balinese Hindu culture, tropical landscapes, warm hospitality, and modern amenities creates a backdrop that is almost unfairly photogenic, the kind of place where pointing your camera in any direction yields content worth sharing.

The island's appeal breaks down into distinct regions, each with its own identity and draw. Ubud, Bali's cultural heart, sits in the island's lush interior surrounded by rice paddies, sacred monkey forests, and artisan villages. It is the epicenter of Bali's wellness and yoga scene, home to world-class retreat centers, raw food cafes, and Balinese craft workshops. Creators like Lost LeBlanc and Kara and Nate have filmed extensively in Ubud, drawn by the dramatic jungle settings and spiritual atmosphere that makes every frame look like a postcard. The Campuhan Ridge Walk at sunrise, the Tegallalang rice terraces, and the Tirta Empul water temple are among the most filmed locations in all of travel content.

Canggu is the polar opposite of Ubud's tranquility and represents the beating heart of Bali's digital nomad revolution. This once-sleepy surf village has transformed into a buzzing hub of coworking spaces, trendy brunch spots, sunset beach bars, and a social scene that runs seven days a week. For content creators, Canggu offers the holy trinity: reliable high-speed WiFi, affordable living costs, and an endless stream of fellow creators to collaborate with. The famous Deus Ex Machina, Finns Beach Club, and Old Man's bar have become landmarks in the creator economy. Surf breaks like Batu Bolong and Echo Beach provide daily content opportunities, while the nearby Tanah Lot temple offers golden-hour magic that has graced millions of Instagram feeds.

Seminyak brings a more polished, upscale energy. This is Bali's fashion and fine dining district, home to boutique shops, international restaurants, and beach clubs like Potato Head and Ku De Ta that have become destinations in their own right. For creators covering luxury travel, Seminyak provides a curated, cosmopolitan backdrop that feels worlds apart from the rice paddies just 30 minutes north. The beach here stretches for kilometers, with dramatic sunsets that rival anything on the Amalfi Coast.

Uluwatu, perched on Bali's dramatic southern limestone cliffs, is where surfers, sunset chasers, and temple devotees converge. The Uluwatu Temple, set atop a sheer cliff face with views that stretch to the horizon, is one of the most-filmed religious sites in Southeast Asia. Below the cliffs, world-class surf breaks like Padang Padang and Bingin attract wave riders from around the globe. The Single Fin bar, built into the cliff face overlooking the surf, has become perhaps the most iconic sundowner spot in all of Bali. Creators covering adventure travel and surfing culture inevitably find their way here.

Beyond these main hubs, Bali hides treasures that keep creators coming back. Nusa Penida, a rugged island off Bali's southeast coast, offers some of Indonesia's most dramatic landscapes: the T-Rex-shaped Kelingking Beach cliff, the natural infinity pool of Angel's Billabong, and world-class manta ray diving at Manta Point. Sidemen, in Bali's east, provides the rice terrace experience without the crowds, set against the towering backdrop of Mount Agung. The waterfalls of central Bali, including the spectacular Sekumpul and the hidden Tukad Cepung (a waterfall inside a cave), offer content moments that feel almost too beautiful to be real. Combined with a food scene that ranges from $1 nasi goreng at a warung to $200 tasting menus at Locavore, Bali delivers an infinite content loop that keeps creators returning year after year.

Things to Do in Bali

From riding waves to chasing waterfalls, these are the experiences that keep creators coming back to the Island of the Gods.

Water Sports

Surfing

World-class waves at Uluwatu, Padang Padang, Canggu's Batu Bolong, and Echo Beach. Bali offers breaks for every level, from gentle beginner waves to barrel-heavy reef breaks that attract the world's best surfers.

350+ videos 15+ breaks
Culture

Temple Tours

Bali is home to thousands of Hindu temples, from the clifftop Uluwatu Temple and ocean-perched Tanah Lot to the sacred Tirta Empul water purification temple and the massive Mother Temple of Besakih on Mount Agung's slopes.

400+ videos 20+ temples
Nature

Rice Terrace Walks

The terraced rice paddies of Tegallalang and UNESCO-listed Jatiluwih are Bali's most iconic landscapes. Walk through centuries-old subak irrigation systems and photograph the emerald-green cascading fields that define the island's visual identity.

500+ videos 5+ sites
Adventure

Waterfall Chasing

Bali's interior hides dozens of spectacular waterfalls. Sekumpul (the most majestic), Tibumana (the most accessible), Gitgit (the tallest), and Tukad Cepung (hidden inside a cave) each offer unique experiences that travel creators cannot resist filming.

250+ videos 30+ falls
Diving

Scuba Diving

Dive with oceanic manta rays at Nusa Penida's Manta Point, explore the USAT Liberty shipwreck at Tulamben, and discover vibrant coral walls in Amed. Bali offers some of the best diving in the Coral Triangle, the most biodiverse marine region on Earth.

180+ videos 10+ sites
Wellness

Yoga & Wellness

Ubud is the global epicenter of yoga and wellness retreats. From the Yoga Barn and Radiantly Alive studios to silent meditation retreats, sound healing ceremonies, and traditional Balinese healing, the spiritual side of Bali is transformative.

200+ videos 50+ studios
Trekking

Mount Batur Sunrise Trek

A 2 AM wake-up leads to one of Bali's most rewarding experiences: watching the sunrise from the summit of an active volcano. The 2-hour trek up Mount Batur (1,717m) rewards hikers with panoramic views of Lake Batur and Mount Agung as dawn breaks.

Nightlife

Beach Club Culture

Bali's beach club scene is legendary. Finns Beach Club in Canggu, Potato Head in Seminyak, and the cliffside Rock Bar at AYANA Uluwatu offer world-class cocktails, international DJs, and sunset experiences that have defined an entire genre of travel content.

When to Visit Bali

A month-by-month breakdown of weather, crowds, prices, and what to expect throughout the year.

☀ Dry Season (April - October)

April - May

Shoulder season with excellent weather, fewer crowds, and lower prices. Rain tapers off. Ideal for budget-conscious travelers and content creators wanting uncrowded shots of popular locations. Temperatures around 27-30C.

June - August

Peak season. Driest months with the most sunshine. Highest tourist numbers and hotel prices. Book 2-3 months ahead for popular resorts. Perfect weather for surfing, trekking, and outdoor activities. The busiest time at beach clubs and restaurants.

September - October

Late dry season and arguably the sweet spot. Great weather, diminishing crowds as European summer ends. Excellent hotel deals start appearing. The surf is still pumping and the rice terraces are golden. Many creators' favorite time to visit.

🌧 Wet Season (November - March)

November - December

Rains begin, typically short afternoon showers. Mornings are often sunny and clear. Significant hotel discounts (30-50% off peak prices). The landscape turns brilliantly green. December picks up for holiday season travelers.

January - February

Wettest months. Heavier rainfall, though rarely all-day rain. Waterfalls are at their most powerful and spectacular. Lowest prices of the year on accommodation. Fewer tourists means authentic cultural experiences. Humidity is higher.

March

Rains begin to ease. The Nyepi (Day of Silence) festival occurs in March, a unique cultural experience where the entire island shuts down for 24 hours. No flights, no lights, no noise. The most unique cultural event in all of Southeast Asia.

Bali Travel FAQ

Everything you need to know before planning your trip to the Island of the Gods.

What is the best time to visit Bali?
The best time to visit Bali is during the dry season from April to October. July and August are the peak tourist months with the driest weather and warmest temperatures. The shoulder months of April-May and September-October offer great weather with fewer crowds and lower prices. The wet season from November to March brings afternoon rain showers but also lush green landscapes and lower hotel rates. For creators, September-October is the sweet spot: great weather, manageable crowds, and golden rice terraces.
How much does a trip to Bali cost?
Bali caters to all budgets. Budget travelers can spend $30-50 per day including a guesthouse room, local meals at warungs, and scooter rental. Mid-range travelers typically spend $100-250 per day for boutique hotels, restaurant dining, and guided tours. Luxury travelers should budget $500-5,000+ per night for five-star resorts like Four Seasons Sayan, Capella Ubud, or The Mulia. Activities like surfing lessons ($20-50), temple tours ($10-30), and scuba diving ($60-150) are generally affordable compared to Western prices. The local currency is the Indonesian Rupiah (IDR), with roughly 15,600 IDR to 1 USD.
Do I need a visa to visit Bali?
Citizens from most countries can enter Bali visa-free for stays up to 30 days, or obtain a Visa on Arrival (VOA) for 30 days which can be extended once for an additional 30 days. The VOA costs approximately 500,000 IDR (around $32). For longer stays, a B211A social visa allows 60 days with extensions up to 180 days. Digital nomads often use the newer B211A Digital Nomad Visa for extended stays. Always check the latest requirements with the Indonesian embassy or immigration website before traveling, as visa policies can change.
What are the best areas to stay in Bali?
Bali has distinct regions for different travel styles. Ubud is the cultural heart with rice terraces, yoga, and art galleries; perfect for wellness seekers and culture lovers. Canggu is the surf and digital nomad capital with trendy cafes and coworking spaces. Seminyak offers upscale beach clubs, boutique shopping, and fine dining. Uluwatu features dramatic cliffside temples, world-class surf breaks, and sunset bars. Nusa Dua is the luxury resort zone with calm beaches. Nusa Penida has dramatic cliffs and manta ray diving. Sidemen is the off-the-beaten-path gem with Mount Agung views and traditional village life.
Why do travel influencers love Bali so much?
Bali is the #1 destination for travel influencers because it offers an unmatched combination of factors: photogenic landscapes (rice terraces, temples, waterfalls, beaches, and volcanic mountains), affordable luxury accommodations that look like a million dollars on camera, fast WiFi and professional coworking spaces for content creation, a thriving international community of fellow creators, diverse content opportunities ranging from surfing to yoga to food to nightlife, year-round warm weather, and a cost of living that allows creators to live well while building their channels. Over 2,400 travel creators have featured Bali, making it the most-documented destination in the creator economy.
Is Bali safe for solo travelers?
Bali is generally very safe for solo travelers, including solo female travelers. The Balinese people are renowned for their warmth and hospitality. Common-sense precautions apply: watch for motorbike traffic (roads can be chaotic), be careful with ocean currents especially at beaches without lifeguards, negotiate prices before taking transport, keep valuables secure, and stay aware of your surroundings at night. The tourist infrastructure is extremely well-established, with reliable healthcare, police presence in tourist areas, and an English-speaking service industry. This makes Bali one of the most beginner-friendly solo travel destinations in all of Southeast Asia.
How do I get around Bali?
The most popular way to get around Bali is by scooter rental, which costs approximately 60,000-80,000 IDR ($4-5) per day. An international driving permit is technically required. Ride-hailing apps like Grab and Gojek offer affordable car and motorbike taxi services. Private drivers can be hired for about $40-60 per day for a car with driver, which is ideal for day trips to waterfalls or temples. Bali does not have a public train or metro system. For inter-island travel, fast boats connect Bali to the Nusa islands and Lombok. Ngurah Rai International Airport (DPS) in southern Bali is the main gateway.
