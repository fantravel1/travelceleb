Why Creators Love Iceland

Fire, ice, and raw natural beauty that defies imagination

Iceland is not simply a destination — it is another world entirely. This volcanic island perched on the edge of the Arctic Circle is a place where tectonic plates pull apart beneath your feet, where glaciers grind slowly over active volcanoes, and where the night sky erupts in curtains of shimmering green and violet light. For travel creators, Iceland offers something no other country on Earth can match: a landscape so dramatic, so alien, and so relentlessly photogenic that every single frame looks like it belongs in a science fiction film.

The northern lights are Iceland's crown jewel and the reason many creators first make the journey. From September through March, the aurora borealis dances across the dark Icelandic sky in waves of emerald, pink, and purple — a celestial spectacle that has generated billions of views across YouTube and social media. But the magic does not stop when the sun returns. Summer brings the midnight sun, when golden light bathes the landscape for nearly 24 hours a day, creating endless shooting opportunities and an ethereal atmosphere unlike anything else in the world.

Beyond the sky, Iceland's terrain is a masterclass in geological extremes. Massive glaciers like Vatnajokull — the largest ice cap in Europe — sit atop active volcanic systems, creating ice caves of luminous blue crystal that form and reshape each winter. The geothermal forces beneath the surface power erupting geysers, bubbling mud pools, and natural hot springs scattered across the countryside. Waterfalls cascade from every cliff face — Seljalandsfoss, where you walk behind a curtain of water; Skogafoss, whose thundering mist creates permanent rainbows; and Dettifoss, the most powerful waterfall in Europe, whose raw fury leaves visitors speechless.

The otherworldly black sand beaches of Reynisfjara, the moss-covered lava fields that stretch to the horizon, the steaming rhyolite mountains of Landmannalaugar painted in ochre, pink, and jade — Iceland delivers visual spectacle on a scale that has made it one of the most filmed countries on the planet. Over 1,200 travel creators and counting have turned their cameras on this extraordinary island, producing more than 500 videos that collectively capture the raw, untamed spirit of a place where nature reigns supreme.

Must-Visit Locations

The regions and landmarks that define Iceland

Reykjavik

The world's northernmost capital is a vibrant base camp for Icelandic adventures. Colorful streets, a thriving food scene, Hallgrimskirkja church, and the Harpa Concert Hall make it far more than just a stopover.

Capital City

Golden Circle

Iceland's most popular route connecting three iconic sites: Thingvellir National Park (where tectonic plates meet), the erupting Strokkur geyser, and the thundering Gullfoss waterfall. Doable as a day trip from Reykjavik.

Must-Do

South Coast

A stunning stretch from Seljalandsfoss and Skogafoss waterfalls to the black sand beach of Reynisfjara, the Dyrholaey arch, and the Vik sea stacks. The most filmed coastline in Iceland.

Photography

Snaefellsnes Peninsula

Called "Iceland in miniature" for its concentration of landscapes — the iconic Kirkjufell mountain, Snaefellsjokull glacier volcano (from Jules Verne's novel), black pebble beaches, and dramatic coastal cliffs.

Scenic

Jokulsarlon Glacier Lagoon

A surreal lagoon filled with floating icebergs calved from Breidamerkurjokull glacier. Nearby Diamond Beach features crystal-clear ice chunks scattered on black volcanic sand — one of Iceland's most iconic images.

Iconic

Landmannalaugar

A highland paradise of rainbow-colored rhyolite mountains, natural hot springs, and vast lava fields. Accessible only in summer via F-roads, this remote area rewards adventurous travelers with some of Iceland's most surreal scenery.

Highlands

Top Activities

The experiences creators love most in Iceland

Northern Lights Hunting

Chase the aurora borealis across the Icelandic countryside from September through March. Guided tours head to the darkest skies away from Reykjavik, while patient solo hunters find magic at Thingvellir, Vik, and Jokulsarlon.

Must-Do $80-150

Glacier Hiking

Strap on crampons and trek across ancient ice on Solheimajokull or Svinafellsjokull glaciers. Guided expeditions navigate crevasses, ice ridges, and moulins while revealing the raw power of Iceland's frozen landscapes.

Adventure $100-200

Whale Watching

Spot humpback whales, minke whales, dolphins, and even blue whales from Husavik (the whale capital of Europe) or Reykjavik's old harbour. Summer months offer the highest sighting rates and midnight sun cruises.

Wildlife $80-120

Hot Springs Soaking

From the iconic Blue Lagoon to hidden wild springs, Iceland's geothermal pools are a way of life. Sky Lagoon, Myvatn Nature Baths, and secret riverside springs offer unforgettable soaking experiences.

Wellness Free-$100

Ice Cave Exploration

Enter crystal-blue ice caves beneath Vatnajokull glacier, available from November through March. Each cave is unique and temporary, sculpted by meltwater and reformed every winter into new luminous formations.

Winter Only $150-250

Snorkeling Silfra

Snorkel or dive between the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates in water so clear visibility exceeds 100 meters. The glacial spring water at Thingvellir is among the purest on Earth.

Unique $130-200

Best Time to Visit

Two distinct seasons, each with its own unforgettable rewards

☀️ Summer (June - August)

  • ✓ Midnight sun — nearly 24 hours of daylight
  • ✓ Warmest temperatures (10-15 degrees Celsius)
  • ✓ Highland F-roads open for exploration
  • ✓ Best for Ring Road and full-island road trips
  • ✓ Whale watching peak season in Husavik
  • ✓ Puffin colonies active (May - August)
  • ✗ No northern lights visible
  • ✗ Peak tourist season with highest prices

❄️ Winter (October - March)

  • ✓ Northern lights season at its peak
  • ✓ Crystal blue ice caves open (Nov - Mar)
  • ✓ Frozen waterfalls and dramatic snow scenes
  • ✓ Fewer tourists and lower shoulder prices
  • ✓ Magical atmosphere and cozy Reykjavik culture
  • ✓ Unique winter photography opportunities
  • ✗ Limited daylight (4-6 hours in December)
  • ✗ Highland roads closed, weather unpredictable

Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know before visiting Iceland

What is the best time to visit Iceland?

It depends on what you want to see. Summer (June to August) offers midnight sun, warmer temperatures (10-15 degrees Celsius), accessible highland roads, and the best conditions for the Ring Road. Winter (October to March) is the season for northern lights, ice caves, and dramatic snow-covered landscapes, though daylight is limited to 4-6 hours. September and March offer a balance of both worlds with a chance for aurora and reasonable daylight.

How many days do you need in Iceland?

Most travel creators recommend 7-10 days to drive the full Ring Road (Route 1) around the island. A minimum of 5 days covers the Golden Circle, South Coast, and Reykjavik. Two weeks allows time for the Westfjords, Snaefellsnes Peninsula, and highland detours. Even a long weekend of 3-4 days can cover the Golden Circle and South Coast highlights.

Is Iceland expensive to visit?

Iceland is one of the most expensive countries in Europe. A meal at a casual restaurant costs 2,500-4,000 ISK (18-30 USD). Hotels average 25,000-50,000 ISK (180-360 USD) per night. Budget travelers can save by camping (available June to September), cooking at guesthouses, and shopping at Bonus supermarkets. Renting a camper van is a popular cost-effective option that combines transport and accommodation.

Do you need a 4x4 vehicle in Iceland?

For the Ring Road (Route 1) in summer, a regular 2WD car is sufficient. However, a 4x4 is essential for F-roads (highland roads), winter driving, and reaching remote locations like Landmannalaugar or Thorsmork. Many rental agreements prohibit 2WD vehicles on F-roads. In winter, a 4x4 is strongly recommended even for paved roads due to snow and ice conditions.
